Part 1

Intro / Basics

15 min

Goal: everyone has enough vocabulary to participate in the activity. Don't try to teach Strudel fully — just enough that $:, ~, *, note(), and .gain() all mean something concrete.

opening "Today we're doing something different — we're going to read code by listening to it. Strudel is a live coding environment for making music in your browser. You write code, hit play, it loops. Change the code while it's running and the music updates on the next loop. Nothing to install."

Walk through each concept card on the basics page (page 1) in order. Paste each snippet into Strudel on the projector, run it, talk about what's happening. About 1–2 minutes per concept.

1. The cycle~2 min

Paste sound("bd hh sd hh"), run it. Then add more sounds and run it again. Point out that it gets faster — everything fits in one loop.

Key idea: "Strudel doesn't care how many sounds you write — it always squeezes them into the same time. More sounds = faster. Fewer = slower."
2. Drums + Silence~3 min

Show bd sd hh oh cp. Run sound("bd sd bd sd"), then sound("bd ~ sd ~"). Hear the difference.

"This tilde is silence. It's not nothing — it's holding a beat. The rests are what make it feel like a beat instead of just noise."
3. Repeat with *~2 min

Run sound("hh*4") then sound("hh*8"). Then combine: sound("bd ~ sd ~") and sound("hh*8") on separate lines won't both play yet — leads into $:.

4. Notes + melody~3 min

Define melody plainly: "a sequence of pitched notes." Run note("c3 e3 g3 e3").sound("piano").

watch outThe pentatonic / "easy mode" notes are c3 eb3 f3 g3 bb3. Don't say "pentatonic" — say "these 5 notes sound good together." Music theory vocab will lose people instantly.
5. Volume — .gain()~1 min

Quick. Show sound("hh*8").gain(0.3) vs no gain. Hats sit behind the kick.

6. The $: layering~3 min

This is the most important concept. Paste the full 3-line example from the basics page and run it. Everything they'll do for the rest of the hour uses $:.

"The dollar-colon at the start of a line is what runs multiple patterns at the same time. Each line is its own independent loop. This is how you layer drums, bass, and melody."
if running long Cut the gain/volume concept — it's intuitive enough that people will figure it out during the activity. Keep silence, repeat, notes, and $:.
Part 2

Level 1 — Edit the Beat

25 min

Everyone starts from the same 3-line skeleton, edits it to make it their own, then one person at a time gets their beat played and the room transcribes by editing their own skeleton to match.

the skeleton
$: sound("bd ~ sd ~")
$: sound("hh*4").gain(0.4)
$: note("c3 ~ ~ ~").sound("piano")
transition "Now you're going to make your own beat — but everyone starts from the exact same starting point. Open level 1 in the link I shared. There's a button that opens the skeleton in Strudel.cc already loaded, or you can copy it. Your job is to edit what's there — not add new lines."
one round — runs ×3 in 25 min
~3 minMake. Everyone edits the skeleton independently. Walk around, help with syntax errors.
~30 secPick. Random selection. Composer plays their beat twice through the speakers. Others can't see the screen.
~3 minRecreate. Everyone else edits their skeleton to match what they heard.
~2 minReveal + discuss. Composer puts their code on the projector. 2–3 others share theirs. Compare.

Discussion prompts after each reveal:

→ What did the room get right — rhythm, pitch, or density?

→ Did anyone use different code to get the same sound?

→ What was hardest to hear accurately?

→ What would you change to make your beat easier to transcribe?

most common issue Someone writes something too complex and the room can't transcribe it. That's fine and actually good for discussion — ask "what made this hard?" and "what constraint would have helped?" Don't treat it as a failure.
if a round goes too fast Stretch the discussion. The reveal is the point — running 3 rounds with rushed discussion is worse than running 2 with real conversation.
if a round goes too slow Skip the third round, jump to Level 2 early. Level 2 is the more interesting activity anyway.
Part 3

Level 2 — Add to the Beat

15 min

Same broken telephone format but the skeleton is a complete groove and people add new $: lines on top instead of editing what's there. The original 3 lines stay untouched.

the skeleton
$: sound("bd ~ ~ ~ bd ~ sd ~")
$: note("c2 ~ c2 ~ eb2 ~ g2 ~").sound("gm_synth_bass_1").gain(0.8)
$: sound("hh*8").gain(0.2)
transition "Level 2 is different. This skeleton is already a complete groove — kick, bass, hi-hats — you can't change any of it. Your job is to add new lines on top. Same broken telephone format: you'll write your addition, someone gets picked, then everyone tries to hear what they added and add the same thing on their own skeleton."
one round — usually fits 1–2 rounds in 15 min
~4 minAdd. Everyone adds 1–2 new $: lines below the skeleton. Page has example ideas if people freeze up.
~30 secPick. Random selection. Person plays their full beat (locked lines + their additions).
~3 minRecreate. Others try to add lines on their skeleton to match the additions they heard.
~2 minReveal + discuss. Show the composer's full code. Compare.

Discussion prompts:

→ Same skeleton, completely different songs — what made each version distinct?

→ What was hardest to hear: the melody, the rhythm, or the instrument?

→ Did anyone add something that changed how the locked lines felt without touching them?

→ What's the minimum addition that makes the biggest difference?

watch out The skeleton has very quiet hats (gain(0.2)) on purpose so they don't compete with what people add. If someone says "I can't hear the hats," that's correct — they're supposed to sit underneath.
if people freeze up Point them at the "what to add — ideas" cards on the page. Each one is a complete $: line they can paste and modify.
Part 4

Close

5 min

Wrap-up. Make sure everyone saves their beat before closing the tab.

closing "Before you close Strudel — copy your final beat somewhere. Paste it back in any time. The take-home links on the basics page point to the official Strudel workshop if you want to go deeper. Thanks for playing along."

Optional final question if there's time: "What's something you noticed today that you'd take into how you read regular code?" Good code reading club closer — bridges the music back to the broader skill.

Reflection

After the session

→What concepts needed more time than expected?

→What discussion question got the most engagement?
→What would you cut or expand next time?
